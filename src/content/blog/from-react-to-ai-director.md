When I told my team I was going to lead our AI initiatives, the first question was: "But you're a frontend engineer?" That's exactly the point.

## The skills that actually transferred

The skills that made me good at React — composability, state management, user empathy — turned out to be the same skills that matter in AI product design.

### Composability

In React, you learn to break complex UIs into composable, reusable pieces. In AI systems, the same thinking applies to prompt chains, agent architectures, and tool compositions.

```typescript
const agent = await createDesignAgent(workflow)
```

### State management

Years of wrestling with Redux and MobX taught me to think about state flows. LLM applications are fundamentally state machines — context windows are state, conversation history is state, tool results are state.

### User empathy

Frontend engineers obsess over UX because they see users struggle in real-time. When building AI products, that instinct to watch users and iterate is the difference between a demo and a product.

## The learning curve

I won't pretend it was smooth. I taught myself enough ML to be dangerous — not to build models from scratch, but to understand what's possible and what's hype.

The hardest part wasn't the technical learning. It was convincing stakeholders that a frontend engineer could lead AI product development. The work spoke for itself.

## Where I am now

Now I lead a 25-person team at Certa, shipping AI products to enterprises. Every day I use the mental models I built during a decade of frontend engineering.

The path from frontend to AI leadership isn't well-documented because most people assume you need a ML background. You don't. You need product sense, system thinking, and the willingness to learn.
