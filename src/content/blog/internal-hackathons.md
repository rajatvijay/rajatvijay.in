Every quarter at Certa, we run "The Automation Cup" — a two-day internal hackathon. Teams of 3-4 people build whatever they want, as long as it touches our product.

## Why it works

Most company hackathons fail because they optimize for output. Ours optimizes for connections.

**Cross-functional teams are mandatory.** An engineer, a CSM, a product person, and a designer. People who wouldn't normally work together discover shared context.

**The judging criteria are weighted toward impact, not polish.** A rough prototype that solves a real customer pain point beats a polished demo that solves a hypothetical.

**Winners get to ship.** Not a trophy. Not a gift card. The winning project gets sprint time to become a real feature. This changes the incentive structure entirely.

## The meta-benefits

After running this for a year, the hackathon itself became less important than what it produced:

- Three features in our current roadmap started as hackathon projects
- Cross-team relationships that made incident response faster
- Junior engineers who found their voice by leading a hackathon team

## How to start one

1. Pick a cadence (quarterly works for us)
2. Make teams cross-functional by default
3. Give the winner real sprint time, not swag
4. Keep it to 48 hours — constraints breed creativity

The Automation Cup costs us two days of sprint velocity. It returns months of cultural momentum.
