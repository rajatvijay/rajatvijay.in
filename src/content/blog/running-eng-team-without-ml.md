"So who on your team has the PhD?"

Nobody. And we're shipping AI products to Fortune 500 companies.

## The credentials myth

There's a persistent belief that leading an AI engineering team requires deep ML expertise. This made sense in 2018 when building AI products meant training models from scratch. In 2026, it doesn't.

The landscape shifted. Foundation models commoditized the ML layer. The hard problems are now:
- **Product design** — what should the AI actually do?
- **System architecture** — how do you make it reliable at scale?
- **User experience** — how do users interact with non-deterministic systems?

These are engineering and product problems, not research problems.

## How I evaluate ML work

I can't review a training run like a staff ML engineer. But I can:

1. **Ask the right questions**: What's the baseline? What's the failure mode? What does the user see when it's wrong?
2. **Read the metrics**: I learned enough statistics to understand precision/recall tradeoffs and evaluation frameworks.
3. **Trust but verify**: I hired people smarter than me in ML and created an environment where they can challenge my assumptions.

## Hiring strategy

When you don't have ML credentials, hiring ML talent requires a different pitch:

- Lead with the **problem space**, not the tech stack
- Show them the **user impact**, not the infrastructure
- Offer **autonomy** on technical decisions

The best ML engineers I've hired told me they joined because I was honest about what I didn't know and clear about what I needed them to own.

## Building trust

Trust comes from results. Ship early, measure everything, and give credit publicly. Within six months, nobody asks about your background anymore — they ask about your roadmap.
